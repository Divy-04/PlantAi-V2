Core Requirements

What every healthy plant needs

These 6 essentials apply to virtually every plant species. Master these fundamentals and you'll prevent most diseases before they even start.

Essential
💧

Water

The right amount at the right time. Most plants prefer deep, infrequent watering over frequent shallow watering — it encourages deeper root growth.

Essential
☀️

Sunlight

Light drives photosynthesis. Know your plant's needs — full sun (6+ hrs), partial shade (3–6 hrs), or shade. Wrong light causes weak, leggy growth.

Foundation
🪨

Soil Quality

Healthy soil is alive. It needs the right pH, good drainage, organic matter, and billions of beneficial microbes to support plant roots.

Climate
🌡️

Temperature

Most plants have a preferred temperature range. Frost kills tropical plants; excessive heat stresses cool-season crops. Know your climate zone.

Often Missed
💨

Air Circulation

Good airflow prevents fungal diseases, strengthens stems, and ensures CO₂ reaches leaves. Overcrowded plants are the #1 cause of disease outbreaks.

Feeding
🧪

Nutrients

Plants need NPK — Nitrogen for leaves, Phosphorus for roots and fruit, Potassium for overall health. Deficiencies show up as yellowing or poor yield.

Watering Guide

Watering — The Most Common Mistake

Both too much and too little water are lethal. Learn to read your plants and water with intention.

💧

Watering Fundamentals

Click any question to expand the answer

There is no single answer — it depends on the plant, pot size, soil type, humidity, and season. The best method is the "finger test": push your finger 2cm into the soil. If it's dry, water deeply. If it's still moist, wait.

  • 🍅 Vegetables (tomato, pepper): Every 2–3 days in summer, deeply
  • 🌳 Fruit trees: Every 7–10 days when established
  • 🌵 Succulents / cacti: Every 2–3 weeks, let soil dry completely
  • 🌿 Herbs: When the top 1cm of soil is dry, usually every 1–2 days

Early morning (6–9 AM) is ideal. The water reaches roots before the heat of the day causes evaporation, and leaves dry out before evening — reducing fungal disease risk.

  • ✅ Best: Early morning before 9 AM
  • ⚠️ Acceptable: Late evening if morning isn't possible
  • ❌ Avoid: Midday watering — high evaporation, leaf scorch risk
  • ❌ Avoid: Overhead watering on foliage — always water at the base

Both overwatering and underwatering cause wilting — but for different reasons. Here's how to tell them apart:

  • 💧 Overwatering: Yellow leaves, mushy stems, root rot smell, soggy soil, mold on soil surface
  • ☀️ Underwatering: Dry crispy leaf edges, soil pulling away from pot edges, leaves feel thin and papery
  • 🔍 Test: Stick finger 2cm into soil — wet = overwatered, bone dry = underwatered
  • 💡 Tip: Most house plants die from overwatering, not underwatering
Disease Prevention

Early Detection & Prevention

Prevention is always cheaper than treatment. Learn to spot disease early and stop it in its tracks.

🛡️

Disease Prevention & Early Detection

Know the signs before they become serious

Early detection is the most powerful tool against plant disease. Check your plants at least twice a week, especially during warm and humid weather.

  • 🔍 Flip leaves over — most pests and fungal spores start on the underside
  • 🟡 Yellow spots or rings on leaves often indicate fungal or viral infection
  • ⬛ Dark lesions or water-soaked spots suggest bacterial disease
  • 🕸️ Fine webbing on leaves = spider mite infestation
  • ⬜ White powdery coating = powdery mildew fungus
  • 📷 Take a photo and use PlantAI to identify it instantly

Chemical fungicides work, but preventing disease naturally is better for your soil, beneficial insects, and the environment.

  • 🧄 Neem oil spray — broad-spectrum antifungal and insecticide, apply every 7–14 days
  • 🥛 Baking soda solution (1 tsp/liter water) — effective against powdery mildew
  • 🫙 Compost tea spray — introduces beneficial microbes that outcompete pathogens
  • 🌻 Companion planting — basil near tomatoes reduces aphids and repels certain fungi
  • ✂️ Prune infected leaves immediately and dispose — never compost diseased material
  • 🔄 Crop rotation — never plant the same family in the same spot 2 years in a row

Chemical fungicides should be a last resort — when natural methods haven't worked and the disease is spreading fast. Always read the label.

  • ⏱️ Apply early in the disease cycle — fungicides prevent spread, not cure existing damage
  • 🧴 Copper-based fungicides are effective for both fungal and bacterial diseases
  • 🌅 Apply in the evening or morning — never in midday heat or strong sun
  • 🧤 Always wear gloves and avoid inhaling spray mist
  • 🐝 Avoid spraying on open flowers — protects pollinators
  • 📅 Rotate between different fungicide types to prevent resistance
Soil & Nutrition

Soil Health & Nutrition

Healthy soil is the foundation of healthy plants. Get the chemistry right and half the battle is already won.

🪨

Soil Health & Nutrition

pH, composting, and nutrient management

Soil pH (0–14 scale) determines how well plants absorb nutrients. Even if your soil has plenty of nutrients, wrong pH "locks" them out and causes deficiency symptoms.

  • 🍅 Tomato, pepper, corn: pH 6.0–6.8 (slightly acidic)
  • 🫐 Blueberries: pH 4.5–5.5 (very acidic)
  • 🌹 Most vegetables: pH 6.0–7.0
  • 🧪 Test your soil with a cheap pH meter or test kit
  • ⬆️ Raise pH (too acidic): add garden lime
  • ⬇️ Lower pH (too alkaline): add sulfur or peat moss

Compost is the single best thing you can add to your garden soil. It improves drainage in clay soils, water retention in sandy soils, and feeds billions of beneficial organisms.

  • ♻️ What to compost: vegetable scraps, eggshells, coffee grounds, dry leaves, grass clippings
  • 🚫 Never compost: diseased plants, meat, dairy, pet waste, oily foods
  • 💧 Keep compost moist but not soggy — like a wrung-out sponge
  • 🔄 Turn it every 2 weeks to speed up decomposition
  • ✅ Ready when it's dark, crumbly, and smells earthy — takes 2–6 months
  • 📏 Apply 5–8cm layer around plants each growing season

Plants show clear symptoms when they're missing key nutrients. Learn to read these signs before they become serious.

  • 🟡 Yellow older leaves, slow growth = Nitrogen deficiency (add compost or balanced fertilizer)
  • 🟣 Purple undersides of leaves = Phosphorus deficiency (add bone meal)
  • 🟤 Brown leaf edges, weak stems = Potassium deficiency (add wood ash or potash)
  • 🟡 Yellow between green veins on young leaves = Iron deficiency (add chelated iron)
  • ⬜ Pale new growth = Magnesium deficiency (add Epsom salt spray)
  • 🔍 Always test soil before adding nutrients to avoid over-fertilizing

Seasonal Care

What to do each season

Your garden calendar — every key task at the right time, so nothing gets missed.

🌸

Spring

The season of new beginnings — prep your soil, start seeds, and set the stage for a productive year

1

Prepare & Amend Soil

Test pH, add compost, till gently to incorporate nutrients before planting

2

Start Seeds Indoors

Begin tomatoes, peppers, and cucumbers 6–8 weeks before last frost date

3

Apply Compost

Spread a 5–8cm layer of finished compost over beds to kickstart the season

4

Prune Dead Branches

Remove winter-damaged wood before new growth begins to direct energy to healthy branches

5

Begin Pest Monitoring

Set up sticky traps, check undersides of leaves weekly as temperatures warm

☀️

Summer

Peak growing season — keep up with water, monitor closely, and harvest regularly to encourage more production

1

Water Deeply & Regularly

In heat, most vegetables need deep watering every 2–3 days. Use the finger test always

2

Mulch to Retain Moisture

A 5–8cm layer of straw or wood chips reduces water loss by up to 50%

3

Monitor for Disease

Warm, humid summers are peak disease season. Check twice weekly and act fast

4

Fertilize Actively

Fruiting plants are heavy feeders — apply balanced fertilizer every 3–4 weeks

5

Harvest Frequently

Regular harvesting signals the plant to produce more — don't let fruit over-ripen on the vine

🍂

Autumn

Wind down wisely — clean up thoroughly to prevent diseases from overwintering and returning next spring

1

Clear Diseased Plant Debris

Remove all infected plant material and dispose — don't compost it, as pathogens can survive

2

Plant Cover Crops

Sow clover or winter rye to prevent erosion, fix nitrogen, and protect soil biology

3

Add Compost to Beds

Let compost break down slowly over winter — it'll be ready for spring planting

4

Collect & Store Seeds

Harvest seeds from your best plants. Dry thoroughly and store in paper envelopes

5

Protect Perennials

Mulch roots of perennial plants to insulate against frost damage

❄️

Winter

The garden rests — use this time to plan, prepare, and set yourself up for an outstanding spring season

1

Plan Next Year's Layout

Sketch crop rotation plans and decide what new varieties to try this year

2

Maintain Tools

Clean, sharpen, and oil all garden tools so they're ready for spring

3

Start Compost Pile

Even in winter, a compost pile breaks down slowly — start one now to have rich compost by summer

4

Prune Dormant Trees

Winter is the best time to prune fruit trees — less disease risk and easier to see structure

5

Order Seeds Early

Best varieties sell out fast — order from seed catalogs by January for the widest selection
